A Compassionate Choice

For many pets, a trip to the veterinary hospital — even one they have visited many times — brings stress, anxiety, and unfamiliar smells and sounds. In their final moments, every comfort matters. In-home pet euthanasia allows your companion to remain on their own bed, surrounded by the people who love them, in the place where they feel safest.

Dr. David Bennett, DVM, is a 1997 graduate of the UC Davis School of Veterinary Medicine and a California-licensed veterinarian with nearly three decades of clinical experience (California Veterinary Medical Board license #12991). He has dedicated his house-call practice to East Bay families and their pets, bringing compassionate, expert end-of-life care to the home — where every pet feels safest. His approach is quiet, gentle, and unhurried. There is no waiting room, no fluorescent lighting, no rush. There is only your pet, your family, and the time you need.

What to Expect During an In-Home Visit

Every family is different, and every visit is shaped to your needs. In general, what you can expect:

  • A quiet conversation first. Dr. Bennett will sit with you and your pet, answer any questions, and make sure you feel ready before anything begins.
  • Gentle sedation. Your pet receives a sedative first, so they relax and drift gently to sleep before the final step. They do not feel pain or distress.
  • A peaceful passing. Once your pet is fully asleep, the final injection is administered. It is quick, gentle, and painless.
  • Time afterward. You may stay with your pet as long as you wish. There is no clock and no need to hurry.
  • Keepsakes, if you wish. A paw print or a small clipping of fur can be prepared as a remembrance.

Knowing When It Is Time

Choosing the moment to say goodbye is one of the hardest decisions a pet owner ever faces. There is rarely a single perfect day — only the day that is right for your pet and your family.

Some signs that a pet’s quality of life has declined may include:

  • Persistent loss of appetite or interest in favorite treats
  • Difficulty standing, walking, or getting comfortable
  • Loss of bladder or bowel control with visible distress
  • Withdrawal from family, hiding, or unusual restlessness
  • Pain that cannot be managed by medication
  • More bad days than good

A Quality-of-Life Framework

Veterinary hospice care has long used a simple quality-of-life framework called the HHHHHMM scale, developed by oncology veterinarian Dr. Alice Villalobos. The seven categories are:

  • Hurt — is your pet in pain that medication does not adequately relieve?
  • Hunger — are they still eating willingly and keeping food down?
  • Hydration — are they drinking, or showing signs of dehydration?
  • Hygiene — can they still keep themselves clean, or are they soiling where they rest?
  • Happiness — do they still respond to you, to favorite toys, to greetings at the door?
  • Mobility — can they get up, walk, and find a comfortable position?
  • More good days than bad — when the scale tips toward more hard days than gentle ones, it may be time.

There is no single right answer in any one of these. Many veterinarians score each category from 1 to 10 and watch the total over time — but for most families, simply walking through the seven categories honestly is enough to clarify where things stand.

If you are unsure, Dr. Bennett is glad to talk with you, examine your pet, and help you weigh the decision — without pressure either way. Sometimes a quality-of-life conversation leads to more comfortable months together. Sometimes it confirms what your heart already knows.

How to Prepare

There is no single right way to prepare for your pet’s passing. What follows are gentle suggestions — use whichever feel right and skip the rest.

Choose a quiet, comfortable place

A favorite dog bed, a cushion on the floor, a soft rug, a sunny patch by a window, your own bed. Anywhere your pet has felt safe and at ease. Avoid hard floors and surfaces they associate with vet visits. If your pet has a favorite spot, that is often the right one.

Have a favorite blanket or toy nearby

The familiar scent of a well-loved blanket, a favorite stuffed animal, or a t-shirt that smells like you can be a quiet comfort. Many families like to have something soft to wrap or cradle their pet in afterward.

Decide who will be present

You decide who is in the room — family members, close friends, the children. Most pets are calmer with the people they love most. If a young child wants to be present, that is often a meaningful experience for them; you know your child best. There are no wrong choices here.

What about your other pets

You may wish to let other pets in the household be present, briefly or throughout. Many believe pets benefit from seeing — or sniffing — a companion who has passed, rather than wondering where they went. If your other pets are anxious or rambunctious, they can be in another room with the door closed.

Food and water

Please do not feel obligated to coax a final meal. Many pets at the end of life are not eating well, and that is normal. If they want a favorite treat or a sip of broth, offer it. If they have no interest, let it be.

Music, candles, photos

Some families like soft music playing. Some light a candle. Some place a few photos nearby. None of these are required and none are unusual. Make the space feel like yours.

Plan for afterward

You may want a quiet evening with no other commitments. Have tissues nearby. A friend or family member who can sit with you afterward, or a meal you do not have to cook, is a kindness to yourself. Grief is exhausting; give yourself room for it.

Aftercare Options

After the visit, Dr. Bennett can coordinate aftercare with trusted local providers. Common options include:

  • Private cremation — your pet’s ashes are returned to you, often in a simple urn or in a vessel of your choosing
  • Communal cremation — ashes are not returned
  • Home burial — permitted in many parts of the East Bay; check your local ordinances

You are welcome to discuss any preferences in advance. There is no wrong choice; there is only what feels right for your family.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does an in-home pet euthanasia visit take?

Most visits take 45 to 60 minutes. Dr. Bennett will not rush you. You set the pace — for the conversation, for the moments before, and for as long as you wish to stay with your pet afterward.

Will my pet feel any pain?

No. Your pet first receives a sedative that relaxes them completely and gives them a deep, comfortable sleep. The final injection is given only after they are fully asleep. They feel only your hand on their fur.

Can my children be present?

That is entirely up to your family. Many parents find that including children — even young ones — helps them understand what has happened and supports their own grieving. Dr. Bennett is gentle and welcoming with children of any age. If you prefer, children can be in another room.

Can my other pets be present?

Yes, and it is often a kindness to them. Pets who are present at the passing of a household companion often grieve more naturally than those who simply find their friend gone. Dr. Bennett can talk you through what to expect.

What if my pet has an accident during the process?

This sometimes happens as the body relaxes under sedation. It is completely normal and not a cause for embarrassment. Dr. Bennett brings absorbent pads and will quietly take care of anything — you will not need to lift a finger.

How should I prepare my home?

Choose a quiet, comfortable spot — a favorite bed, a sunny patch by a window, a soft rug on the floor. Have your pet’s favorite blanket or toy nearby. Decide ahead of time who in the family will be present, and whether your other pets will be in the room. There is no wrong setting; only what feels right for your pet.

Can you come on evenings or weekends?

Dr. Bennett does his best to accommodate families during difficult moments. Standard hours are weekdays and Saturday mornings, but please call (925) 519-2316 if you need an evening or Sunday visit — he will do what he can.
